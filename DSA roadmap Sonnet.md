## ⚔️ SECTION 2 — THE DEPTH FORGE: Hard Topics Battle Plan

> These are the topics where most candidates give up. You will not.
> Each entry has: **when it appears in the roadmap**, **why it's hard**, and **what elite mastery looks like**.

---

### 🔴 TIER 1 — HARDEST (Master These or Fail Hard Rounds)

#### Dynamic Programming (Weeks 11–12)
**Why it's hard:** State definition is non-obvious. Transition functions have subtle edge cases. Space optimization requires understanding the recurrence direction.
**What mastery looks like:**
- You can define the state for any problem within 3 minutes of reading
- You derive bottom-up from top-down mechanically
- You can space-optimize any 2D DP to O(n) or O(1) where applicable
- You recognize DP disguised as greedy or graph problems
**Hard problems to specifically conquer:** Burst Balloons, Cherry Pickup II, Palindrome Partitioning II, Regular Expression Matching, Stone Game series, Digit DP, DP on Trees

#### Graphs — Advanced (Week 10)
**Why it's hard:** Multiple algorithms, each with subtle implementation details. Choosing the right algorithm requires deep understanding.
**What mastery looks like:**
- You implement Dijkstra, Kruskal, Prim, Kosaraju, Tarjan without notes
- You model unusual problems as graph problems (state-space graphs, implicit graphs)
- You know every failure mode (negative cycles in Dijkstra, etc.)
**Hard problems to specifically conquer:** Word Ladder II, Number of Ways to Arrive at Destination, Critical Connections (Bridges), Minimum Cost to Connect Points

#### Backtracking + Pruning (Week 8)
**Why it's hard:** Generating the right state space tree and pruning efficiently requires creative thinking.
**What mastery looks like:**
- You can code N-Queens, Sudoku Solver, and Word Search under time pressure
- You prune aggressively — your solutions are optimal, not brute force
- You know when backtracking is wrong and DP is right
**Hard problems to specifically conquer:** Expression Add Operators, Palindrome Partitioning, M-Coloring, Word Break II

---

### 🟠 TIER 2 — VERY HARD (Appear in Final Rounds)

#### Monotonic Stack / Deque (Week 5)
**Why it's hard:** The pattern is non-intuitive. Maintaining monotonicity while tracking indices is error-prone.
**What mastery looks like:** You recognize NGE/NSE/SumOfSubarrayMins instantly. You solve Largest Rectangle, Trapping Rain Water, and Maximal Rectangle without hints.

#### Segment Trees / Binary Indexed Trees (⚡ Bonus — Week 12 extension)
**Why it's hard:** Implementation is intricate. Range queries with updates are conceptually new for most.
**What mastery looks like:** You implement a Segment Tree with lazy propagation. You use BIT (Fenwick Tree) for range sum and range update queries.
**Note:** This is a BONUS topic — if you've completed everything else, add this in the final week.

#### Union-Find / DSU (Week 10)
**Why it's hard:** The optimizations (path compression + union by rank) together achieve inverse-Ackermann time, which most candidates cannot explain.
**What mastery looks like:** You implement DSU in 15 lines. You apply it to cycle detection, Kruskal's, number of connected components, and redundant connections instantly.

#### KMP / Z-Algorithm (Week 3)
**Why it's hard:** The failure function construction is unintuitive. Most candidates memorize it without understanding.
**What mastery looks like:** You derive the failure function from scratch and explain *why* it works.

## ⚡ PROBLEM-SOLVING PROTOCOL (For Every Single Problem)

1. **Read once** — understand what is being asked
2. **Think before typing** — minimum 5 minutes of dry thinking
3. **Brute force first** — state the O(n²) or O(n³) solution out loud
4. **Optimize** — ask: can I use sorting, two pointers, binary search, a hash map, a stack, or DP to reduce?
5. **Code** — clean, commented, edge-case aware
6. **Test** — at least 3 test cases before submitting
7. **If stuck for 30 minutes** — look at the approach/hint only, not the full solution
8. **After solving** — read the editorial anyway. There is always something to learn.
